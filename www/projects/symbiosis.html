Symbiosis

DURATION

Jan 8, 2021 - Mar 12, 2021

ORGANIZATION

ForestHealthWatch

FIELD(S)

UX Research, UX Design

TOOLS

Figma, Miro

ROLES/RESPONSIBILITIES

Individual: Conducting interviews, open-coding transcriptions, v1 and v2 prototypes

Collaborative: Affinity analysis, design requirements

Method

In collaboration with Lyle Hamm, Linh Bui, and Laura Munoz of the University of Washington, I researched, designed, and prototyped “Symbiosis,” an app that encourages people to interact with and appreciate nature.

How can we inspire more people to connect with nature and be invested in the health of their local ecosystem?

Context

ForestHealthWatch monitors Western Red Cedar dieback as an indicator for the effects of climate change in the Pacific Northwest. Currently, however, they only have about 100 volunteers collecting this data. Therefore, our goal for this project was to ideate gamification concepts that could inspire more people to be more invested in their local ecosystem and get involved with projects such as ForestHealthWatch.

User Research

We first conducted four semi-structured interviews with young adults who enjoy going outdoors to explore how people currently interact with nature when outdoors and what motivates them to go outdoors. *Names are in pseudonyms

Derek

Derek loves to go outdoors when it is with his friends or family. He finds the outdoors refreshing and uses the opportunity to disconnect himself from technology.

"I love going on hikes or walking with friends."

Eric

Eric aims to step outside at least once a day to refresh himself when he is feeling stressed. He finds nature to be meditative and restorative.

"I grew up a foot away from a national park... so nature is very important."

"Sometimes I feel obligated to take pictures. But I prefer to enjoy the scenery."

Sarah

Sarah likes to engage in some sort of structured activity when she goes outside. Recently, she likes to use the Nike running app to track her distance as she runs, which helps her feel accomplished.

"Running - people don't bother me. I don't have to talk to people."

"I use the Nike Run Club app - it gets me out more often because it gamifies it."

Shaun

Shaun is very active, going on morning runs every day to keep himself fit. He carries his Apple watch to track his distance and time.

"I don't have to think very much. I could just listen to music and go out any time I want."

"It's nature, so I feel like it's healing my body."

Data Analysis

After we each revisited our interview transcripts and marked significant details with open coding, we used affinity analysis to group and categorize insights. Key insights shared among the participants include:

  • Varying attitudes toward the use of technology when outdoors, but all were eager to “disconnect from the network” (e.g., social media)
  • Participants were motivated by having some sort of structured activity to engage in when outdoors
  • Being with friends and family improves the outdoor experience

Design Requirements

Based on our insights, we came up with the following design requirements that support our participants’ desires and motivations:

  1. Allow "disconnecting" from the network - interactions should not involve social networks
  2. Promote physical, social interactions
  3. Provide explicit motivations for going outside (quests)

Ideation

We then each sketched six ideas surrounding a social activity that promotes being present with nature. After synthesizing and dot voting on design ideas, we decided to proceed with a gamified nature app with quests and a focus on community.

Prototype Version 1

I quickly designed a low-fidelity prototype of Symbiosis in Figma and asked for feedback from ForestHealthWatch and peer designers about the design's desirability and aesthetics.

Key pieces of feedback I received:

  • Add more features that support socialization, as according to interviewees, it was a key motivator for going outside.
  • Consider how the app would connect to ForestHealthWatch
  • Consider how the concept of symbiosis can be reflected more in the app

Final Prototype

Rationale for major design features marked on image:

  1. Providing badges to work toward incentivizes continuous engagement with the app and motivation.
  2. Knowing that people have different preferences for technology use outdoors, I give users the option to not participate in the activity if desired.
  3. Challenges focus on mindfulness to encourage users to connect with nature.
  4. Connecting with friends and comparing collections for social motivation.
  5. Interested users can click "Read more" to find activist groups for specific plants/trees.

Results

The prototype was met with positive feedback from ForestHealthWatch, who were inspired to implement some of our concepts embedded in the design:

  • Incentivize participation in data collection with rewards (e.g., gift cards)
  • Provide space for collaboration/competition as social motivations
  • Gamify data collection for unmapped areas

Reflection

What I learned:

  • Importance of initial scoping of project: it was difficult to prototype concrete approaches to meet participant needs/desires.
  • Surprisingly, participants already felt a spiritual connection to nature when outdoors.
  • Importance of considering feasibility in the final prototype: although ForestHealthWatch took inspiration from our ideas, many concepts in our designs proved to be too complex to implement.

If I did this again, I would...

  • Communicate the importance of considering feasibility in the ideation phase to my teammates.
  • Do another round of user research after narrowing down research goals.
  • Analyze existing products that do similar things (e.g., iNaturalist) to see how we can address gaps in their approaches.
